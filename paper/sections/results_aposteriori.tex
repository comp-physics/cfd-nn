\section{A Posteriori Evaluation}
\label{sec:aposteriori}

To assess whether improved offline accuracy translates to better coupled CFD predictions, we evaluate all 15 models within the solver on four canonical flow configurations spanning separated flow over curved surfaces, bluff-body vortex shedding, turbulence-driven secondary flow, and three-dimensional wake dynamics.
Each model uses the same spatial discretization, time integrator, and Poisson solver; only the turbulence closure differs.
The cost data for every model--case combination was reported in Section~\ref{sec:cost}; here we focus on prediction accuracy and use the cost data to construct a Pareto cost--accuracy tradeoff in Section~\ref{sec:pareto_plot}.

For clarity, figures and tables in this section highlight a representative subset of six models spanning all closure classes: baseline (laminar), SST $k$--$\omega$, EARSM-WJ, MLP ($32^2$), TBNN-small ($32^2$), and TBRF (1 tree).
The complete 15-model results are tabulated in Appendix~\ref{app:full_aposteriori}.

\subsection{Square duct at $\Rey_b = 3{,}500$}
\label{sec:aposteriori:duct}
% =====================================================================

Turbulent flow in a square duct generates Prandtl's secondary flow of the second kind: streamwise-aligned vortices driven by Reynolds stress anisotropy near the duct corners.
This secondary flow is absent in any isotropic (Boussinesq) closure and can only be predicted by models that produce anisotropic Reynolds stresses.
The reference is the DNS of \citet{Pinelli2010} at $\Rey_b = 3{,}500$.

The duct geometry was included in the training data (14 Reynolds numbers, Section~\ref{sec:training}), making this a trained-geometry evaluation.
However, the a~posteriori test probes a capability the a~priori evaluation cannot: whether models trained on RANS fields with near-zero cross-plane velocities (the $k$-$\omega$ SST cannot predict secondary flow) can, when coupled to the solver, generate the secondary motion from the anisotropic stress correction alone.
During training, the DNS labels contain nonzero $b_{13}$ and $b_{23}$, but the RANS inputs have near-zero $V$ and $W$; in the coupled solver, the predicted anisotropic stresses drive cross-plane velocities that were absent in the training input---a feedback loop not tested offline.

\subsection{Sphere at $\Rey = 200$}
\label{sec:aposteriori:sphere}
% =====================================================================

Flow past a sphere at $\Rey = 200$ is axisymmetric and steady, with a well-defined separation point on the sphere surface and a recirculating wake.
This three-dimensional, unseen geometry provides the most demanding test of model generalization, requiring the closure to operate on velocity gradients that differ qualitatively from the two-dimensional training data.
The reference is the DNS of \citet{Johnson1999}, which reports $\bar{C}_d \approx 0.77$ and a separation angle of $\theta_\mathrm{sep} \approx 117^\circ$.
